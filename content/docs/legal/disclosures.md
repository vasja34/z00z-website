# Disclosures

> [!warning]
> **Maturity:** `Site legal target`
>
> **Reading rule:** Disclosures are where the site says out loud what a reader must not infer. They should reduce ambiguity, not hide behind vague warning language.

Z00Z needs explicit disclosures because the project sits at the intersection of privacy, settlement, external assets, governance, and future-facing rights design. Without a disclosure layer, readers will fill the gaps themselves and often assume more maturity, endorsement, or legal certainty than the corpus supports.

## The Main Disclosure Buckets

| Disclosure area | Why it matters | What the docs should say clearly |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Maturity | The corpus mixes live direction with target architecture. | Separate current protocol posture from future lanes and research surfaces. |
| Token and economics | Readers may assume revenue, value, or market claims that the project is not entitled to make. | Keep incentives, tokenomics, and treasury language bounded and non-promotional. |
| Third-party services | Wallets, bridges, auditors, or operators may exist outside the core project. | Make it clear when a service is independent rather than protocol-native. |
| Issuers and external assets | External assets create their own trust and legal questions. | Avoid implying endorsement, redemption promises, or official listing status. |
| Communications posture | Public claims can turn architecture papers into unsupported guarantees. | Point readers back to the claim-boundary rules when a statement could be misread. |

## Why The Disclosure Layer Is Not Optional

The whitepapers do not treat disclosures as a cosmetic footer. They treat them as part of architectural honesty. A privacy-first system can still be misunderstood if it speaks too broadly about token value, legal posture, or service maturity. Disclosures correct that by keeping the reader aware of where a claim ends.

This is especially important for pages about tokenomics, cross-chain rights, useful-work programs, and external integrations. Those topics are inherently interesting, which means they are also where overstatement tends to happen first.

## Good Disclosure Behavior

Good disclosures do three things well:

- they identify the exact kind of misunderstanding they are trying to prevent;
- they stay close to the page they qualify instead of hiding in a distant appendix;
- they use precise boundary language instead of dramatic but empty warnings.

For example, a strong disclosure says that an external asset issuer owns its own redemption and compliance posture. A weak disclosure says only that "risk exists." The first one is actionable. The second one is theater.
